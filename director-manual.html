1.3 Market & Strategic Position

⚡ Director Priority
Myanmar's vehicle fleet is growing rapidly, with motorcycle tyres representing the largest volume segment. Local production offers a significant cost advantage over imports. The factory's strategy should focus on capturing domestic demand growth while expanding into the higher-margin truck and agricultural tyre segments.

Key market dynamics (update figures from latest industry reports in the Industry Literature folder):

  • Fleet operators (buses, logistics companies) are priority B2B targets for contracted supply agreements.
  • Export to neighbouring markets (Bangladesh, Laos, Cambodia) is a medium-term growth direction to explore.

2.2 Rubber Compounding

Compound formulation is the core intellectual property of any tyre manufacturer. The compound recipe determines rolling resistance, wet grip, durability, and cost. As Director, you must understand the trade-offs:

Sourced from Thailand and Myanmar. Key grade: RSS1, TSR20. Controls: Mooney viscosity (ML 1+4 @ 100°C), dirt content, volatile matter. High NR content → better cut resistance and heat build-up in truck tyres. Price is commodity-driven — hedge with 90-day forward contracts.

SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber): Blended with NR for tread compounds — improves wet grip and abrasion resistance. BR (Butadiene Rubber): Used in sidewalls and tread base — reduces rolling resistance and heat generation. Both are imported; monitor USD/barrel correlation for price forecasting.

Carbon black is 25–35% by weight of tread compound. Grade N330 (HAF) for truck tyres, N550 for sidewalls. Silica-filled compounds offer superior wet grip and lower rolling resistance but require silane coupling agent — higher cost, longer mixing cycle. Consider silica for passenger car premium range.

Sulphur + accelerators (CBS, TBBS, TMTD) form crosslinks during curing. The crosslink density determines hardness (Shore A target: tread 60–68, sidewall 54–62). Cure time and temperature are critical process controls — deviations directly cause under/over-cure defects.

5.3 Inventory Management Policy

Safety Stock = Z × σLT × √(Lead Time)
Where Z = 1.65 for 95% service level, σLT = demand standard deviation during lead time.

Reorder Point (ROP) = (Average Daily Usage × Lead Time) + Safety Stock

The inventory management system automatically calculates and monitors ROP for all tracked raw materials and triggers alerts when stock falls below threshold.

All raw materials are issued on FIFO basis. Each lot has a unique barcode scanned at receipt and at mixing. The DQMS maintains full traceability: finished tyre → production batch → compound lot → raw material lot → supplier CoA. This enables rapid recall if a field issue is traced to a specific material batch.

6.4 Working Capital Management

Working capital is the lifeblood of a manufacturing business. A profitable factory can still run out of cash if it mismanages its working capital. As Director, you must understand the cash conversion cycle and manage it actively.

⚡ Core Concept: Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO

  • DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding) = (Average Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365 — how many days your cash is locked in raw materials and finished goods.
  • DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) = (Accounts Receivable ÷ Revenue) × 365 — how many days before customers pay you.
  • DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) = (Accounts Payable ÷ COGS) × 365 — how many days before you pay your suppliers.

Goal: Keep CCC low. A CCC of 30 days means you recover cash 30 days after buying raw materials. A CCC of 90 days means cash is tied up for 3 months — requiring working capital loans and paying interest.

LeverActionWho Owns It
Reduce DIOImprove demand forecasting; avoid over-stocking raw materials; reduce finished goods by matching production to orders; JIT receiving for short-lead-time materialsSupply Chain Manager + Production Manager
Reduce DSOTighten credit terms (e.g. Net 30 max for distributors); collect overdue invoices promptly; offer small early-payment discount to key customersFinance Manager + Sales
Increase DPONegotiate longer payment terms with key suppliers (45–60 days); consolidate purchasing to increase negotiating power; only extend DPO for financially stable suppliers — don't stress small suppliersSupply Chain Manager + Finance Manager

Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
A higher number is better. For a tyre manufacturer, aim for 8–12× per year on finished goods. Raw materials should turn faster (monthly deliveries = ~12×).

Stock-out Cost vs Overstock Cost: Stock-outs cost you lost production and customer penalties. Overstock costs you interest on tied-up cash and risk of expiry/obsolescence. The sweet spot is the reorder point formula in Chapter 5.

Slow-moving inventory: Any raw material or finished good sitting >90 days should be flagged. Write down its value in your financial accounts and investigate the root cause — wrong forecast, cancelled order, or quality issue.

6.5 Pricing Strategy & Contribution Margin

Pricing is one of the most powerful profit levers — and one of the most misunderstood. Many factory directors focus on revenue (top line) without understanding which products actually contribute to profit. This section gives you the tools to price correctly and identify your most profitable products.

⚡ Key Formula: Contribution Margin

Contribution Margin (CM) = Selling Price − Variable Costs per unit

Variable costs include: raw material per tyre + direct labour per tyre + energy per tyre + packaging per tyre.

Contribution Margin % = (CM ÷ Selling Price) × 100

Fixed costs (depreciation, management salaries, factory rent) don't change with volume — so a product with high CM% is more profitable as volume grows. A product with low CM% may destroy value even when you're busy.

The minimum price floor is the point where you cover all variable costs AND a contribution to fixed costs. Never accept an order below variable cost — you lose money on every unit. The formula:

Price Floor = Raw Material + Direct Labour + Energy + Packaging + Minimum Contribution

Example structure: If variable costs = 80% of your selling price,
then: Selling Price = Total Variable Cost ÷ 0.80
(i.e. if your variable cost per tyre is $8, the minimum price is $8 ÷ 0.80 = $10)

The contribution above variable cost must cover: factory depreciation, management salaries, interest, and profit. Work backwards from your target net margin.

StrategyWhen to UseRisk
Cost-plus pricingStandard approach: variable cost + fixed cost allocation + target profit margin. Simple and ensures coverage. Best for commodity products (motorcycle tyres).Ignores competition — may price too high or too low vs market.
Value-based pricingSet price based on value to customer (e.g. truck tyres — longer tread life = lower cost per km for fleet operator). Premium price justified by premium performance.Requires proof of performance data; customers may not believe claims without track record.
Contract/volume pricingFleet customers get volume discount in exchange for annual purchase commitment. Lower per-unit price but guaranteed volume improves production planning.Locked-in price when costs rise. Build cost-escalation clauses into long-term contracts.
Competitive pricingMatch or beat competitor prices to win a new segment. Only viable if your cost structure supports it — use contribution margin analysis first.Price wars destroy industry profitability. Compete on price only as a temporary market-entry tactic, not as a long-term strategy.

Break-Even Volume = Total Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin per unit

This tells you the minimum number of tyres you must sell to cover all fixed costs. Below this volume, you are making a loss. Above it, every additional unit generates profit equal to its contribution margin.

Break-Even Revenue = Total Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin %

Calculate this for each product line separately. It tells you which products "pay for themselves" fastest and which ones need volume to justify production.

📌 Director Application
Before launching a new product or accepting a major order at a discounted price, always ask: does this pass the break-even test? Does the additional volume contribute enough to fixed costs without undermining your pricing in other segments?

6.6 Capital Investment Decisions

As Director you will face decisions on major capital expenditure: new machinery, capacity expansion, new product lines, ERP systems, factory upgrades. These frameworks help you evaluate them rigorously.

Payback Period = Capital Investment ÷ Annual Cash Saving (or Annual Net Profit from investment)

Example: A new curing press costs $200K and saves $60K per year in downtime and rework costs → Payback = 200 ÷ 60 = 3.3 years.

Rule of thumb for manufacturing equipment: payback ≤ 4 years is generally acceptable. Payback > 6 years requires strong strategic justification beyond pure financial return.

Weakness: Ignores the time value of money and cash flows beyond the payback point. Use NPV for larger decisions.

Net Present Value (NPV) discounts future cash flows back to today's value using a discount rate (your cost of capital, typically 12–20% for a Myanmar manufacturing business).

NPV > 0 → investment creates value. NPV < 0 → investment destroys value at the given discount rate.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the discount rate at which NPV = 0. If IRR > your cost of capital, invest. If IRR < cost of capital, don't.

Practical steps for any major investment decision:

  1. Define the investment cost (capital + installation + training).
  2. Estimate annual cash benefits (cost savings, additional revenue, avoided costs).
  3. Estimate the useful life of the asset (typically 7–15 years for factory equipment).
  4. Calculate payback period and NPV (use Excel or a finance calculator).
  5. Include a "downside scenario" (what if benefits are 30% lower than estimated?).
  6. Compare alternatives — always evaluate at least 2 options before deciding.

COPQ is the full cost of not getting quality right the first time. It is typically 10–25% of revenue in a factory that lacks a mature quality system — much larger than most directors realise.

COPQ CategoryExamplesHow to Quantify
Internal failureScrap tyres, rework, sorting, retesting after repairUnits scrapped × material cost + rework labour hours × hourly rate
External failureCustomer warranty claims, returns, recalls, transport to collectNumber of claims × average cost per claim + any goodwill cost
AppraisalInspection labour, QC equipment, lab testing, AQL samplingQC team salary + equipment depreciation + third-party testing fees
PreventionTraining, process improvement projects, calibration, DQMS systemInvestment in prevention activities — these reduce internal and external failure costs
📌 Director Rule
A common quality management benchmark: every $1 spent on prevention saves approximately $10 in internal failure costs and $100 in external failure costs. The exact ratios vary by industry and context — the key principle is that prevention is always far cheaper than failure. Track your own factory's actual COPQ split to understand your specific ratios and improvement opportunity.

8.4 Director's Leadership & Management Toolkit

Technical knowledge alone does not make an effective factory director. Leadership — how you make decisions, motivate teams, manage crises, and continuously improve the organisation — is equally critical. This section distils the most practical frameworks for factory leadership.

Lean manufacturing (derived from the Toyota Production System) is the dominant framework for reducing waste and improving flow in factories. As Director, you need to understand these principles to lead continuous improvement.

PrincipleWhat It MeansApplied to Tyre Factory
5S Sort, Set-in-order, Shine, Standardise, Sustain. Foundation of factory discipline — everything has a place and everything is in its place. Tools and moulds always in the right location → no time wasted searching; clean machines → defects spotted faster; daily 5S audit by shift leaders.
PDCA Cycle Plan → Do → Check → Act. The universal continuous improvement loop. Never implement a change without a plan and a check. OEE improvement: Plan (define target, root cause), Do (trial one shift), Check (did OEE improve?), Act (standardise if yes, revise if no).
Kaizen Small, continuous improvements by everyone, every day. Kaizen events are focused 3–5 day workshops to tackle a specific problem. Operators suggest improvements to mould change process → reduce changeover time from 45 to 30 minutes → OEE improves by 3%.
Andon / Visual Management Make the status of every machine and process visible at a glance. If something is wrong, it should be visible immediately — not hidden in a report. Production boards showing actual vs target units per hour at each station; red/green light on curing presses; defect counters visible to operators in real time.
Poka-yoke Mistake-proofing: design processes so errors are impossible or immediately detected. The best quality control is one that prevents defects, not just detects them. Curing press that won't close unless bladder pressure is correct; barcode scan required before material can be issued to production (prevents wrong compound).
Value Stream Mapping A visual map of every step in the process from raw material to customer delivery — showing value-added time vs. waiting/waste time. Use to identify the biggest time waste in your production flow (often it's waiting time between curing and QC, or finished goods waiting for dispatch). This becomes your improvement priority.

A Director who does everything themselves creates a bottleneck. Your job is to create a system where managers are empowered to act within clear boundaries, and you focus on strategy and exception management.

Delegation LevelManager ActionDirector Role
Level 1 — RoutineManager decides and acts independently. Reports outcome at weekly meeting.Review weekly; adjust KRA if pattern changes. Don't micro-manage routine decisions.
Level 2 — SignificantManager recommends and acts after brief Director confirmation (same-day response).Quick validation: Is it within budget? Does it conflict with another department? Then approve or adjust.
Level 3 — StrategicManager presents analysis and options. Director decides after proper review.Major capex, new supplier contracts, pricing changes, hiring above a certain grade.
Level 4 — Director-onlyDirector handles directly: no delegation.Critical safety incidents, customer escalations at the highest level, regulatory issues, media/government matters.

Setting accountability: Every action agreed in a meeting must have: (1) a named owner, (2) a specific deliverable, (3) a deadline. No meeting should end without these three elements for each agreed action. Review open actions at the start of every meeting.

Three types of factory crises and how to respond:

Crisis TypeFirst 15 MinutesFirst 2 HoursFirst 24 Hours
Safety incident (injury) Stop work in affected area. Ensure casualty receives immediate medical help. Do not move injured person unless danger. Call emergency services if serious. Secure the scene (don't disturb evidence). Notify HR and owner/chairman immediately. Begin witness statements. Issue a "do not operate" on affected machine. Formal incident report. Root cause investigation started. Regulatory notification if required (Labour Department). Full communication to all staff. Review similar risks in other areas.
Major quality escape (defective tyres reached customers) Get the facts: which product, which batches, how many units, what is the defect and safety risk level. Do not guess — get the production and QC records. Quarantine all affected batches in your warehouse. Contact your key customers who have these tyres. If safety risk: initiate recall discussion. Inform owner/chairman. DQMS batch traceability report to identify full scope. Customer communication plan. Root cause analysis started. Corrective action plan with timeline. Legal review if needed.
Critical machine breakdown (production stop) Confirm the scope: one machine or whole line? Maintenance team on it. Estimate time to repair — is it hours or days? Communicate to customers if delivery will be affected (before they chase you). Evaluate options: shift to other machines? Expedite spare parts? Bring in external repair contractor? If >24h downtime: formal customer communication with revised delivery dates. Review spare parts holding for this machine type — this was a gap in your maintenance planning. Update preventive maintenance plan.
📌 Crisis Communication Principle
Communicate early, even when you don't have all the facts. Saying "We are aware of an issue and investigating — we will update you in 2 hours" is far better than silence. Customers and owners can handle bad news; they cannot handle surprises.
  • Make performance visible: KPIs on a board in the factory, not hidden in a spreadsheet. People improve what they can see.
  • Recognise good performance publicly: Name and thank teams or individuals who hit a target, solved a problem, or suggested an improvement. Recognition costs nothing and builds culture.
  • Address poor performance early and directly: A manager who consistently misses KRAs without consequence destroys team morale. Performance conversations should be clear, respectful, and documented.
  • Distinguish system failure from individual failure: If the same mistake happens repeatedly, it is likely a process problem, not a people problem. Fix the process before blaming the person.
  • Invest in your top managers: Give them stretch projects, external training, and exposure to senior decisions. If they leave because you didn't develop them, that is a management failure.
  • Lead by example on safety: If the Director doesn't wear PPE on the factory floor, why would anyone else? Your behaviour sets the standard for everything.

As Director you negotiate regularly — with suppliers on price and terms, with customers on contracts, with banks on financing, with the owner on budgets. These principles apply across all contexts.

  • Know your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement): Before any negotiation, know what you will do if you reach no deal. Your BATNA sets your walk-away point. Without it, you have no leverage.
  • Know their BATNA too: A supplier who has no other large customer in your region has a weak BATNA. A supplier who can sell to 20 other factories has a strong one. This determines who has power.
  • Separate people from the issue: Negotiate firmly on price and terms, but treat the other party with respect. You will deal with these people again. Burning a relationship over a small concession is poor strategy.
  • Always get it in writing: Verbal agreements are worth nothing. Every negotiated outcome — price, volume, delivery date, payment terms, quality standard — must be in a signed contract or written purchase order.
  • Anchor first: In price negotiations, the party who names a number first (the anchor) sets the frame. If buying, anchor low. If selling, anchor high. Always justify your anchor with data (cost breakdown, market price, delivery cost, etc.).
  • Create value before claiming it: In supplier negotiations, explore what value you can offer them (volume commitment, early payment, referrals) before asking for a price reduction. Trades are more sustainable than pure price wins.

Glossary & Quick Reference

This appendix is the Director's quick-lookup reference. Bookmark this page — it covers the essential terminology, formulas, and contacts needed to navigate any meeting, audit, or production review with confidence.

9.1 Tyre Industry Glossary

TermDefinition
AQLAcceptable Quality Level — the worst tolerable process average for sampling inspection (e.g. AQL 1.5 = max 1.5% defective in acceptable lot).
Aspect RatioSidewall height ÷ section width × 100. A "65" in 205/65R15 means sidewall is 65% of 205mm. Lower ratio = sportier handling.
BeadHigh-tensile steel wire bundle that locks the tyre to the wheel rim. Critical safety component — bead failure causes instant deflation.
Banbury MixerInternal batch mixer that combines rubber, carbon black, and chemicals at ~150°C with counter-rotating rotors. Core of the compounding department.
BladderInflatable rubber bag inside the curing press mould that shapes the tyre from the inside during vulcanisation. Lifespan ~500 cycles.
BRButadiene Rubber — synthetic elastomer giving low rolling resistance and high resilience. Used in tread base and sidewalls.
CalenderingProcess of applying rubber coating to fabric or steel cord between heavy rollers to produce ply sheets.
CarcassThe structural skeleton of the tyre — the cord plies that run from bead to bead and carry the load.
Carbon Black (CB)Reinforcing filler made of near-pure carbon. Increases tyre strength 4–5× vs unfilled rubber. Gives tyres their black colour.
CoACertificate of Analysis — supplier document confirming a material lot meets specification. Required for DQMS material release.
CompoundA formulated rubber mixture (elastomers + fillers + chemicals) ready for processing. Each tyre component has a unique compound recipe.
Cure / VulcanisationThe heat + pressure process that forms chemical crosslinks between rubber chains, transforming soft compound into tough, elastic rubber.
DQMSDigital Quality Management System — SuperMega's integrated platform for real-time quality data capture, AI defect detection, and corrective action tracking.
ECEUnited Nations Economic Commission for Europe — issues tyre safety regulations (R30 for car tyres, R54 for truck tyres) required for European market.
EPDMEthylene Propylene Diene Monomer — rubber with excellent ozone and UV resistance. Used in sidewall compounds.
TermDefinition
FIFOFirst In, First Out — inventory management rule ensuring oldest stock is used first. Critical for natural rubber (shelf life: 2 years).
FlashThin rubber fin that forms at mould parting lines during curing. Minor cosmetic defect — trimmed in finishing.
Green TyreThe uncured assembled tyre before it enters the curing press. Soft and sticky — must be cured within 8 hours of building.
GrooveContinuous channel in the tread pattern for water evacuation. Minimum legal groove depth varies by country (typically 1.6mm).
HalobutylChloro- or bromobutyl rubber — used in the inner liner for excellent air impermeability. Key for tubeless tyres.
IoTInternet of Things — sensors on factory machines that transmit real-time data (temperature, vibration, pressure) to the SuperMega platform.
ISO 9001International standard for Quality Management Systems. Certification requires documented processes, management review, and continuous improvement.
KerfNarrow slot in the tread mould blade that creates sipes. Controls wet grip and noise.
Mooney ViscosityStandardised measure of rubber flow resistance (ML 1+4 @ 100°C). Controls processing behaviour — too high = processing difficulty; too low = shape retention issues.
TermDefinition
NRNatural Rubber — from Hevea brasiliensis latex. High tensile strength and cut resistance. Grade RSS1 (premium) or TSR20 (standard). Commodity-priced.
OEEOverall Equipment Effectiveness = Availability × Performance × Quality. World-class benchmark: 85%. Formula: (Good Units × Ideal Cycle Time) ÷ Planned Production Time.
PCIPost-Cure Inflation — inflating the hot tyre immediately after curing press opens to prevent distortion as it cools. Critical for uniformity.
PlyA layer of rubber-coated fabric or steel cord. Radial tyres have radial plies (90° to tread); bias tyres have diagonal plies.
PPMParts Per Million — quality metric for defect rate. 1000 PPM = 0.1% defect rate. World-class automotive target: <50 PPM.
RadialTyre construction where ply cords run perpendicular (radially) to the direction of travel, topped by a steel belt package. Better fuel economy and handling vs bias.
ROPReorder Point — inventory level that triggers a purchase order. Formula: (Average Daily Usage × Lead Time) + Safety Stock.
SBRStyrene-Butadiene Rubber — main synthetic tread rubber. Better wet grip and abrasion resistance than NR. Price tracks crude oil.
SidewallThe flexible side section of the tyre between tread and bead. Contains tyre markings and must resist ozone cracking and flexing fatigue.
SipeFine cut in tread blocks that improves wet traction and snow grip by creating additional biting edges.
TBMTyre Building Machine — the machine that assembles all tyre components (ply, belt, tread, bead) into a green tyre.
TreadThe road-contact zone of the tyre. Its compound and pattern determine grip, wear rate, noise, and rolling resistance.
TWITread Wear Indicator — moulded rubber bar at 1.6mm groove depth. When tread is flush with TWI, tyre is legally worn out.
UniformityConsistency of tyre dimensions and stiffness around its circumference. Poor uniformity causes vibration and uneven wear. Measured on a uniformity machine (force variation test).
